Background

Curious Technologies was inventing a smart-connected device to detect head impacts and potential concussions in youth football players. As their first product idea to use new wearable device technology, Curious Technologies sought a mobile app design from DESIGNATION in December 2015.

Challenge

In a team of 2 UX designers, we had 3 weeks and no financial budget to identify the device’s key users and design an accompanying iOS mobile app.

Week 1 | Research & Synthesis

As our clients focused on building the device, we set out to understand the technology before exploring the domain of concussions, high school football and activity wearables.

Research Goals

  • Explore the scope of concussions and youth sports
  • Gather insights on the goals, needs, and pain points of those involved in assessing athlete head injuries on the sideline
  • Determine users’ expectations regarding impact and concussion data

We explored Savvo's business, grocery store wine consumers, and the wine and food pairing landscape to gain a deeper understanding of this domain.

Domain Insights

  • Concussions are based on the angle and speed of impact, and repeated head impacts overtime increase an athlete’s risk of sustaining one
  • Everyone responds to a head injury differently and athlete monitoring post-impact is crucial to diagnosis as symptoms could take days to appear

  • Only about half of high schools have access to athletic trainers and around 1 in 5 youth athletes sustains a concussion during their sports season
  • Football coaches are mandated to annually complete the CDC’s “Heads Up” online training on concussion detection signs

  • Head impact sensors in the market are geared toward alerting athletes for significant impacts
  • The current technology is either expensive, not focused on youth football, or has really poor interfaces for data visualization

Based on our domain and market exploration, we assumed that wearables were new technology that high schools weren’t aware of or couldn’t afford to have. So, we set out to test our initial thoughts by interviewing stakeholders and subject matter experts.

Learning more about the concussion epidemic made me consider so many directions to take this app, but I focused on the problem at hand by interviewing user after user to learn what the most frustrating parts of concussion safety were to them. From our combined research, we synthesized our data to find patterns that led to defining our personas and their problem.

To bring clarity and focus to our collected data, we each contributed our research insights and then synthesized together by mapping patterns in our findings.

Sythesize research findings with affinity mapping

Key Research Takeaways

  • Athletic trainers would be the primary users as they manage athlete safety and injury rehabilitation. Coaches would be secondary in the absence of one.
  • There isn’t a universal threshold for head impacts, each athlete is affected differently
  • Assessing a head impact is based purely on subjective data, focused on how the athlete feels post-impact and more accurate when the first responder knows the player
  • An athlete’s head impact history or hit data isn’t readily available on the sideline

Synthesizing data helps me to review and process everything I learn from research and it’s this reflective exercise that allows me to truly empathize with end users. We shared the patterns in our findings together to solidify the problem we’d solve and identify the users and their scenarios that would be at the center of our decisions going forward.
Based on our interviews, we developed 3 personas and context scenarios that represented trending user types and use cases.

Problem
With such a strong dependence on subjective information, first responders could benefit from more concrete data. This objective data can offer a better understanding of where and how the head was affected by an impact and supplement first responders’ sideline assessments, ultimately increasing the safety of players and improving return to play decisions.

Design principles

  • Supplementary - aid in concussion assessment and monitoring
  • Assistive - act as an extra set of eyes so the user doesn’t miss a beat
  • Streamlined - align with existing concussion assessment protocol and feel familiar to users
  • Customizable - cater to each user’s unique goals and styles
  • Customizableshare information at the user’s discretion

Week 2 | Ideation

For me, the time after research synthesis and before design ideation is when I re-align business goals with user needs. I’m deliberative with concepting so while I get creative and think big, I also consider practicality and feasibility so that my designs are set up to succeed.
Presenting our research findings to Curious Technologies helped us align the product’s functionality with user needs. As it was a developing technology, the engineers appreciated the direction that our insights brought about what users wanted.

Before we individually sketched solutions for the mobile app, we set the key product functionalities based off research and feasibility.

Key functionalities

  • Impact detection/alert Shareability Impact history SCAT assessment Linear acceleration and rotational force Tackling Technique Performance Measurement

Keeping the design principles of supplementary and assistive in mind, I used task flows to help me organize the feature set in the most assistive and least obtrusive way for first responders–especially because this would be a new technological resource in a potentially resistive market.

Week 3 | Iteration & Refinement

In our final week, Focusing on the needs of our personas and their contexts helped us decide what would make it to the final design: Live mode – when device is “in-use”, this mode allows unobtrusive impact monitoring of hits above an athlete’s custom threshold Customizable threshold: unique g-force setting for each athlete Shareability function - allows continuity in paper keeping among systems Team Dashboard - player roster, athlete profile, impact profile, notes Impact Profile - details impact history with an ability to replay the hit

With my live mode feature selected, I focused on refining those screens to flow with the rest of the app. To help, I created an app map so that we could all see the overall navigation flow of how each screen connected before taking the wireframes into Sketch. Personal Reflection
Going into this project, I was excited to dabble in service design and improve the interaction between grocery shoppers and the kiosk display by taking a holistic approach to problem solving. I learned how much I enjoy helping to make products more usable and also how I can successfully tackle new domains with the help of design-thinking research. If I could do anything differently, it would be to have more access to grocery shoppers at the store and maybe usability “taste” the wine and cheese myself. I’m impressed by how much was accomplished in just 3 weeks and this project helped solidify my transition from being a customer-advocate to a user-centered designer.
In the future, I’d want to spend more time understanding the product’s capabilities once the engineers finish building it and also get access to even more users to test assumptions and explore concepts further. Next big thing in wearables and smart-connected devices
