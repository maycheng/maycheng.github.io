Background

Curious Technologies was inventing a smart-connected device to sense head impacts and detect potential concussions in youth football players. In December 2015, they sought DESIGNATION out to design the native mobile app for one of the first products to use Intel's new sensor technology.

Challenge

In a team of 3 UX designers, we had no financial budget to identify the device’s key users and design an accompanying data visualization mobile app.

Week 1 | Research & Synthesis

With a product that was still being developed, we set out to familiarize ourselves with its technology and explore the domain of concussions, high school football, and activity wearables, before doing exploratory interviews.

Research Goals

  • Learn how concussions are assessed and monitored among youth athletes
  • Identify how wearable technology might fill data gaps to help diagnose concussions
  • Determine potential users and their needs and expectations regarding concussion data

Since the mobile app would display information from the sensor, we set out to understand how its technology could be used to visualize concussion-related data. We used what Intel had released about their new Curie Module and scientific research publications on adolescent concussions as a start.

Insights

  • Concussions are based on the angle and speed of impact
  • The device’s accelerometer and gyroscope can track linear acceleration and rotational force
  • Repeated head impacts overtime increase an athlete’s risk of sustaining one
  • Head impacts affect each person differently and post-impact monitoring is crucial to diagnosis as symptoms could take days to appear

With high school football as our target audience, we needed to understand the domain of concussion safety in youth sports. I took charge of researching the current landscape and the rules of competition. To truly shift perspectives, I even went through the same Center for Disease Control-mandated concussion safety training as high school football coaches to empathize with stakeholders.

Insights

  • A concussion can only be diagnosed and an injured athlete cleared to play by a certified medical professional (doctor)
  • High school athletes sustain 300,000 concussions per year, 64.8% of those happen in football
  • Current assessment tools (use varies by school): imPACT, SCAT, BESS
  • Football athletes were recently limited to 90 minutes of full contact a week (including games)

With our validation of the concussion issue in youth sports, we analyzed the activity wearables market to understand the space. I focused specifically on concussion device wearables to help our clients with their entry into the market. I charted out the sensor functionality and data capabilities of other devices to help.

Insights

  • Head impact sensors in the market were geared toward alerting athletes for significant impacts, with only a few having software to monitor and track data for an athlete’s injury history
  • The current technology was either priced expensively ($80-200), not focused on youth football, or had poor interfaces for data visualization

Based on our domain and competitive market research, we assumed that wearable devices were newer technology that high schools either weren’t aware of or couldn’t afford to have. To test these initial assumptions, we were able to conduct phone and video interviews with 15 high school athletic staffers.

Our goal with exploratory interviews was to gather insights on the needs and pain points of stakeholders involved in assessing athletes on the sidelines. To empathize with each type of player in that space, we divided the interviews among us so that we each talked to an athletic trainer, football coach, parent, and athletic director.

For me, user interviews are a time when I can learn from the expert: the end user. I focused on listening to my interviewees’ perspectives on contact sports, feelings about concussion safety, role in assessing injuries, and their needs and frustrations with the current landscape.

Athletic Trainer Insights

  • Goal: keep athletes safe and rehab their injuries
  • Pain point: cannot attend every sport or watch every player, evaluation is based on subjective data from athlete, pulling an athlete from play/being overly cautious can affect trainer-coach relationship
  • Knowing athlete better helps make more effective assessments
  • Concussion thresholds are not universally applicable to every athlete, which is why what happens post-impact is the most valuable dataset for athletic trainers


Football Coach Insights

  • Goal: train athletes in proper tackling technique to not sustain injury
  • Coaches tend to err on the side of caution, given potential liabilities
  • Trust athletic trainer’s judgment and without access to one:
    • Decision becomes binary: when to bench or when to refer to doctor
    • Some go through checklist of symptoms; some “just know”
    • No medical release = no return to play
  • Concussion safety starts with “safe tackling”


Parent Insights

  • Goal: children enjoy sports, build friendships and learn life lessons
  • Aware about long term impact of repeated head injuries and concussions
  • In general, participation and athleticism benefits of sports outweigh the risk involved


Athletic Director Insights

  • Goal: provide service to community through athletics
  • Manage equipment ordering and budget
  • Implement state concussion protocols
  • Stay on top of new technology, updated protocols and changing regulations nationwide.


Learning more about the concussion epidemic made me consider so many directions to take this app, but I focused on the problem at hand by interviewing user after user to learn what gaps in concussion safety could be filled with our product. From our combined research efforts, we synthesized the data together by affinity mapping trends to create personas and use cases, as well as define our problem to solve and the principles to design for in the ideation phase.

Key Research Takeaways

  • Athletic trainers would be the primary users as they manage athlete safety and injury rehabilitation. Coaches would be secondary in the absence of one.
  • There isn’t a universal concussion threshold for head impacts, each athlete is affected differently by linear acceleration and rotational force
  • Assessing a head impact is based on subjective data (self-reporting), focused on how the athlete feels post-impact and more accurate when the first responder knows the player
  • An athlete’s head impact history or hit data isn’t readily available on the sideline

Based on the trends from our data we developed 3 personas and scenarios that represented patterns in user types and use cases for our device.

Our synthesis wrapped up with defining the problem our app would solve and the principles we’d design by based on the insights we gathered from our stakeholder interviews.

Problem
With such a strong dependence on subjective information, first responders could benefit from more concrete data. This objective data can offer a better understanding of where and how the head was affected by an impact and supplement first responders’ sideline assessments, ultimately increasing the safety of players and improving return to play decisions.

Design Principles

  • Supplementary - aid in concussion assessment and monitoring
  • Assistive - act as an extra set of eyes so the user doesn’t miss a beat
  • Streamlined - align with existing concussion assessment protocol and feel familiar to users
  • Customizable - cater to each user’s unique goals and styles
  • Confidential-share information at the user’s discretion

Week 2 | Ideation

At the end of our first week, we presented the research findings to Curious Technologies, which helped us align user needs with the product’s planned functionality. The product engineers valued direction from our stakeholder feedback and considered adding some feature sets based on the research we gathered.

Before we individually sketched solutions for the mobile app, we set the key product functionalities based off research and feasibility.

Key Functionalities

  • Impact detection/alert
  • Shareability
  • Impact history
  • SCAT assessment
  • Linear acceleration and rotational force
  • Tackling Technique
  • Performance Measurement

During the second week, we sketched concepts individually and exchanged feedback collectively, before each selecting one full direction to prototype for our clients.

In search of iOS design examples for data visualization patterns, I explored mobile app interfaces and UI kits to guide my visual design and even created a mood board for creative inspiration.

Keeping the design principles of supplementary and assistive in mind, I white boarded task flows to help me organize the feature set in the most assistive and least obtrusive way for first responders–especially because this would be a new technological resource in a potentially resistive market.

I sketched a few ideas and ended up wireframing a concept similar to the native iOS stocks app where a trainer could use the landscape feature for an expanded view of a detected impact’s details. To me, it was the least intrusive and most assistive way for our personas to use the app on the sidelines because it didn’t involve any taps.

We used Sketch to build our wireframes and then Proto.io to rapidly prototype so that we could present our clients with an interactive design of our concepts (mine is the first one below).

Trying to maximize end user input as much as possible, we were able to utilize some design colleagues for concept validation testing and heuristics evaluations before presenting our work and the feedback to our client at our weekly meeting.

Week 3 | Iteration & Refinement

Our clients were impressed with each of our concepts so we took that feedback along with our testing insights to create one mid-fidelity wireframe that encompassed all of our strongest design elements. Focusing on the needs of our personas and their contexts helped us decide what would make it to the final design:

  • Live mode – when device is “in-use”, this mode allows unobtrusive impact monitoring of hits above an athlete’s custom threshold
  • Customizable threshold: unique g-force setting for each athlete
  • Shareability function - allows continuity in paper keeping among systems
  • Team Dashboard - player roster, athlete profile, impact profile, notes
  • Impact Profile - details impact history with an ability to replay the hit

With my live mode feature selected, I focused on refining those screens to flow with the rest of the app. To help, I created a quick app map so that we could set the information architecture and navigational flow of how each screen connected before building our mid-fidelity screens.

When it came to refining our deliverables, I lead the final review of all of our screens and created the iconography in Illustrator, while my colleagues annotated our wireframes and built the final interactive prototype.

Outcome

Final Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

Final Prototype

We were able to usability test the prototype with 4 athletic trainers and the response was extremely positive.

Users thought our mobile app had everything that they needed to supplement their sideline assessments and they even considered learning more about the role of g-force in impact assessments to complement their current knowledge of sideline evaluations.

Next Steps
Our client was impressed with the depth of our research insights and how the data was visualized in our final mobile app design. By helping Curious Technologies identify their key users and empathize with stakeholders’ sideline assessment needs, the device was able to incorporate features that the product engineers wouldn’t have known to include without our user research. Our wireframes were used by user interface designers to finish the mobile app that is currently being built by iOS developers.

Personal Reflection
I was excited to dabble in interaction design and work on potentially the next big thing in wearable technology. I learned how to breakdown and prioritize complex data sets to help end users make sense of what’s most important in a seamless way. For future projects, I will only get more efficient at learning new/unfamiliar technologies and domains to help me design their digital counterparts. I’m impressed with how much was accomplished in just 3 weeks and this project helped me hone user-centered design principles in my process-driven way of solving problems.
